A Biography of W.E.B. Du Bois










Early Life


Born on 23 February 1868 in Great Barrington, Massachusetts, William Edward Burghardt Du Bois was an African American activist who played a major role in the American Civil Rights movement. Du Bois reported the local news as a child. He graduated from Great Barrington High School in 1848, becoming its first graduate and valedictorian.



Du Bois then received a bachelor’s degree in law, philosophy and history at Fisk University (a historically black university in Nashville, Tennessee) in 1888.



He then received a bachelor’s degree (cum laude) in 1890, his masters in 1891 and a PhD in 1895 in history from a Harvard university in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Thus, Du Bois was the first African American to earn a PhD from Harvard, with a dissertation of the African slave trade. During his summers, Du Bois taught classics and modern languages at Wilberforce University in Ohio for 2 years: a job he accepted in the summer of 1894. He then married Nina, a student at the university, in 1896.





Accomplishments



Work in Philidelphia and the 'Philidelphia Negro'
In 1896, Du Bois accepted a 1-year research job, working as an assistant in sociology in the University of Pennsylvania. He then moved to the Seventh Ward with his wife. Du Bois had no office on campus, little interactions with its faculty and no contact with its students. Despite these challenges, on 1st August, Du Bois commenced the first scientific study done on race, in which he interviewed 5,000 black Philadelphians in the Seventh Ward. He documented the social problems they faced in the Philadelphia Negro, which was commissioned by the university and published in 1899.



The Souls of Black Folk
Du Bois later wrote The Souls of Black Folk. Published on 18th April 1903 by A.C McClurg and Co., its 51 pages consist of several essays on race (some of which already had been published in the Atlantic Monthly). The seminal highlights the harmful effects of racism on blacks, emphasizing economic inequality, self-hatred and political disenfranchisement. It also advocated for academic integration, enfranchisement and enhanced political power for African Americans.




The NAACP
Additionally, Du Bois was one of the cofounders of the NAACP (National Association for the Advancement of Colored People), which began on 12th February 1909. He was the organization’s director of publicity, starting the NAACP’s official publication, called ‘The Crisis’ in 1910, which documented current affairs in inter-racial relations, especially those concerning African Americans.







Later Life and Impact on the Civil Rights Movement


Later life
In 1934, Du Bois resigned from the NAACP as he believed "separate but equal" was an acceptable position for blacks, a stance that conflicted with the organisation’s views. Later in 1961, upon the invitation of President Kwame Nkrumah, Du Bois moved to a government house in Accra, Ghana. This was to become the editor of a book he conceived the idea of: Encyclopaedia Africana, which documented the history, culture and people of Africa.


W.E.B Du Bois then died on 27th August 1963. He was buried in W.E.B Du Bois Memorial Centre for Panafrican Culture in Ghana’s capital.


Impact on the Civil Rights Movement
Du Bois had a profound impact on the civil rights movement. He publicised the harmful effects of segregation in literatures like The Philadelphia Negro and advocated for African American justice in works like The Souls of Black Folk. Furthermore, the NAACP, which he co-founded, passed several legislations to desegregate housing, employment, and voting (such as the Civil Rights Act of 1957, the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965, and the Fair Housing Act of 1968) as well as enforcing integration in southern schools. Encyclopaedia Africana also echoed the importance of African culture and history, combating popular racist narratives. Overall, Du Bois propelled the civil rights movement into a greater momentum, galvanised activists and helped secure better rights for African Americans.
